Here we attach all the results obtained throughout the development of Freyja. This encompasses two main areas:

  • Experimentation results. Related to effectiveness, we include specific scores for P@K and R@K for all systems on all benchmarks and for all the tested values of K (we also include MAP@K, which is not reported in the paper). As for efficiency, we include specific preprocessing and execution times for all systems on all benchmarks, including our synthetic variants to tests scalability. All of these results are plotted for easier visualization
  • Statistical analysis. In order to guarantee the statistical rigor of our results, we conducted thorough significance testing following the Null Hypoth-esis Significance Testing (NHST) framework.
    • Efficiency analysis. Statistical analysis of preprocessing times was not feasible due to the prohibitively high computational costs of repeatedly executing the preparation stage for competing systems, which can take hours or even days per benchmark (the substantial differences reported provide clear evidence of Freyja’s efficiency advantages). For query execution time, we performed N = 30 independent runs for each system-benchmark pair and applied one-way ANOVA tests, which rejected the null hypothesis of equal means across all benchmarks (p < 0.0001). Tukey’s HSD post-hoc tests demonstrated that differences between Freyja and SANTOS/D3L are highly significant (p < 0.0001), while differences with embedding-based systems (DeepJoin, KGLiDS, WarpGate) were not statistically significant. All query times in Table IV are reported with 95% confidence intervals.
    • Effectiveness analysis. For effectiveness metrics (P@k and R@k), we employed the Wilcoxon signed-rank test, a non-parametric alternative to paired t-tests suitable for bounded metrics that may not follow normal distributions. These tests were conducted across query columns for each benchmark. The results confirmed that performance differences between Freyja and competing systems are statistically significant across nearly all benchmarks (Freyja, D3L, TUS Small, TUS Large, OMCG, OMCR). Notable exceptions where differences were not significant include initial ranking positions (k = 1, 2) in SANTOS Small when compared against Starmie, WarpGate, KGLiDS, and DeepJoin, and k = 1 in TUS Large against DeepJoin (Precision) and Starmie (Recall). These results validate that the variation in effectiveness scores reflects genuine differences in system performance rather than random execution factors.
    • Multiple comparisons control. Given that our evaluation involved 804 statistical tests across multiple benchmarks, metrics, and system comparisons, we addressed the inflated risk of Type I errors (false positives) by applying the Benjamini-Hochberg (B-H) procedure to control the False Discovery Rate (FDR). With an FDR level of Q = 0.05, the resulting significance cutoff was determined to be p ≤ 0.0455. After B-H correction, 765 tests (95.15% of all tests) remained statistically significant, confirming that the expected proportion of false discoveries among our significant results is controlled at or below 5%. This validation strengthens the robustness of our experimental conclusions and demonstrates that Freyja’s performance advantages are not artifacts of random variation but reflect genuine improvements over state-of-the-art approaches.

C. False Discovery Rate (FDR) Correction

This section details the results of the multiple comparison adjustment applied to the statistical tests performed across all benchmarks and metrics. To control the expected proportion of false positives resulting from performing numerous statistical comparisons, we applied the Benjamini-Hochberg (B-H) procedure. This procedure was conducted by aggregating all p-values obtained from the Tukey's HSD tests (Query Time) and the Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (Effectiveness P@k/R@k) into a single list. By choosing a standard False Discovery Rate level Q = 0.05, the resulting cutoff p-value for significance was determined to be 0.0455. In total, 765 tests (95.15% of all tests) were declared statistically significant after B-H correction, confirming the robustness of the derived conclusions.
